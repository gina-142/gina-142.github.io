Case Study 2: Star Trek and the Warp Drive

Star Trek: Alcubierre's Inspiration (Almost) Reality: Alcubierre's Drive An Exotic Problem

The Warp Drive

Have you ever wondered how far it would be possible to travel in our universe? Or how fast you could travel? This has been a problem of much of modern science fiction. If we have already discovered and explored everything within the reachable universe, how would our characters meet new challenges?

In Star Trek, huge ships, like the Star ship Enterprise, are able to ‘warp’ to speeds much faster than the speed of light. When the universe is so big, travelling even close to the speed of light would not get you very far, so like much of science fiction, they came up with a way of bypassing this limit on scale and speed.

The ‘warp drive’ is a form of interstellar propulsion, first introduced in the 1957 novel ‘Islands of Space’ written by John W. Campbell, but most remember it from its popularisation in Star Trek, beginning in 1966. It is described in Star Trek as generating a ‘warp bubble’ around the ship, from a high energy source, which distorts space-time. It can be thought of as squashing space time together, meaning the ship does not have to physically travel as far and not break the universal speed limit, but to an outside observer, the ship appears to have travelled incredibly fast across a huge distance. It is roughly analogous to how worm holes allow travel across the universe by bending space-time (see the Interstellar case study).
