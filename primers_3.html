The tyranny of special relativity

The final tyranny of spaceflight is time dilation. This is explored in depth in the context of warp drives in the Star Trek section, but for completeness, we will discuss it briefly here.

Einstein's postulates

In 1905, the so-called Annus Mirabilis, Einstein published three groundbreaking papers. One on the photoelectric effect - a phenomena with far-reaching consequences that would lay down the groundwork for quantum theory - another on Brownian motion, and one entitled On the electrodynamics of moving bodies.

This would prove to be foundational for our modern interpretation of time.

Assuming two key postulates, which experimental analysis support:

- That the laws of physics are the same for all inertial (constant velocity) observers

- That the speed of light is the same for all observers - if you move towards a beam of light the speed at which you perceive it to be approaching doesn't change

Einstein uncovered deeper truths about the nature of time. Namely, that it is relative, much like position.

Time dilation

A consequence of these postulates, which may be determined by appeal to Pythagoras and relatively straightforward algebra, is that time elapsed for a moving person is different to that of a stationary observer. It is the concept, not the mathematics that is tricky.

The relationship is as follows:

\[ t = \gamma t_0 \]

Where:

  • \( t \) = time elapsed for a stationary observer
  • \( t_0 \) = "proper" time elapsed by the moving person
  • \( \gamma \) = the so-called "Lorentz factor"

The Lorentz factor is defined as:

\[ \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}} \]

Where:

  • \( v \) = is the velocity of the moving person
  • \( c \) = is the speed of light

This reciprocal relationship (essentially gamma being 1 divided by something) means that as \( v \) approaches \( c \), the Lorentz factor tends to infinity. It gets arbitrarily large.

The consequence is that, as one's speed approaches the speed of light - which is essentially the cosmic speed limit - the Lorentz factor becomes huge, and so the time elapsed by the stationary observer gets longer and longer, proportionally to the time elapsed by the traveller.

A space traveller may therefore travel at high speeds and across vast distance, but at the expense that the universe ages dramatically, during what may feel like a very short period of travel.

This is before we even consider the dramatic energy costs that such speed would require.

Time dilation in science fiction

Science fiction creates workarounds, like the Star Trek warpdrive. But, in other media, such time-dilation is an integral part of the plot. This is evidenced in the Queen song, '39, one of the few songs that Brian May - himself an astrophysicist - is the lead vocallists.

Science fiction is generally happy to work around these constraints by creating novel technologies. This is no better exemplified by Star Trek's warp drive or Star Wars' hyperspace.

However, some media exploits this fact of nature and leverages it as a narrative tool.

'39 by Queen

Queen's song, '39, was written by their guitarist, Brian May - who also holds a PhD in astrophysics. It is one of the few songs on which he is the lead vocallists. Before we discuss further, we urge you strongly to give it a listen.

It is heavily inspired by May's astrophysics background and depicts time dilation. The song relates the tale of a group of astronauts who embark on a year-long journey, close to the speed of light. Upon their return, however, everyone on Earth has aged.
