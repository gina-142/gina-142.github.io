Case Study 3: Interstellar — Wormholes and Throat Radius

Synopsis What Is a Wormhole? Geometry and Cost Physics and Fiction

Where Physics and Fiction Meet

Historical context: wormholes in science fiction

Long before Interstellar, science fiction regularly used “shortcuts through space” to solve the narrative problem of interstellar distance. Early stories described tunnels, gates, or hyperspace corridors without committing to a specific physical mechanism. As general relativity became more widely known outside specialist circles, the Einstein–Rosen bridge gradually provided a scientific vocabulary for similar narrative ideas: a path between distant regions of spacetime that avoids the long journey in between7.

In much earlier science fiction, such devices functioned primarily as narrative convenience; their stability, energy requirements, and geometry were rarely interrogated in detail. By the late twentieth century, however, wormholes had become a recognisable concept across literature and screen. Novels such as Contact are particularly notable because the fictional mechanism was shaped through direct consultation with physicists: Kip Thorne advised Carl Sagan that a wormhole provided a more theoretically consistent solution than sending characters through a black hole89. Television franchises including Stargate and Babylon 5 further cemented wormholes as familiar visual shorthand for rapid interstellar travel7.

Interstellar therefore enters a landscape in which wormholes were already culturally familiar. What distinguishes it is not the originality of the idea, but the degree to which the film makes its credibility depend on whether the geometry, lensing, and constraints can be traced back to real general relativity. It inherits a long science-fiction tradition, but attempts to “pay” for the shortcut using established physics.

Interstellar and real physics: mutual influence

Interstellar’s significance lies not only in what it shows on screen, but in where those ideas come from. The wormhole is not an invention of the film; it emerges from a line of thinking that began with Einstein’s description of gravity2, continued through decades of theoretical work, and was developed more rigorously by Morris and Thorne4. Rather than creating new physics for dramatic effect, the film adopted existing theory and accepted its constraints1.

Those constraints are non-trivial. Although general relativity permits wormhole geometries mathematically, maintaining a macroscopic, traversable throat would require forms of negative energy density not known to exist in nature4. No empirical evidence for such structures has been observed. The film does not resolve these difficulties; instead, it treats the wormhole as an artificial object placed near Saturn, implicitly acknowledging that its existence would require intervention beyond currently understood astrophysics.

The influence also moved in the opposite direction. In order to visualise curved spacetime accurately, the production relied on detailed relativistic ray-tracing calculations that later formed the basis of published research6. The relationship between physics and fiction was therefore not one-sided. The theory shaped the story, and the demands of the story required the mathematics of gravitational lensing to be implemented with unusual precision.

Interstellar does not present a wormhole as effortless or inevitable. It presents it as something permitted by equations that have been tested, debated, and refined for over a century. In doing so, it illustrates that scientific development and imagination are not opposing forces, but part of the same ongoing conversation, one in which fiction can clarify theory just as theory constrains fiction.
