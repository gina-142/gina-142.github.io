Case Study 3: Interstellar — Wormholes and Throat Radius

Synopsis What Is a Wormhole? Geometry and Cost Physics and Fiction

Where Physics and Fiction Meet

Wormholes in science fiction

Long before Interstellar, science fiction regularly used the concept of shortcuts through space to solve the narrative problem of travelling across large interstellar distances. Early stories described tunnels, gates, or hyperspace corridors without committing to a specific physical mechanism. But as the science progressed and general relativity became more widely known outside of specialist circles, the Einstein–Rosen bridge gradually provided a scientific vocabulary for similar narrative ideas: a path between distant regions of spacetime that avoids the long, and time-consuming, journey in between.

In much earlier science fiction, the rough concept of the wormhole was exclusively used as a narrative loophole; their stability, energy requirements, and geometry were rarely acknowledged. And by the late twentieth century, wormholes had become a recognisable concept across literature, film and television, but as more than just a narrative device. Novels such as Contact are particularly notable because the novel was shaped through direct consultation with physicists, Kip Thorne advised Carl Sagan that a wormhole provided a more theoretically consistent solution than sending characters through a black hole7. Television franchises including Stargate and Babylon 5 further cemented wormholes as a familiar idea for rapid interstellar travel.

Interstellar therefore entered a landscape in which wormholes were already culturally familiar. What distinguishes it is not the originality of the idea, but the degree to which the film makes its credibility depend on whether the geometry, lensing, and its constraints can be traced back to real general relativity. It inherits a long science-fiction tradition, but attempts to consider the caveats using established physics.

How physics shaped Interstellar (and vice versa)

Interstellar’s significance lies not only in what it shows on screen, but in where those ideas come from. The wormhole is not an invention of the film; it emerges from a line of thinking that began with Einstein’s description of gravity2, continued through decades of theoretical work, and was developed more rigorously by Morris and Thorne4. Rather than creating new physics for dramatic effect, the film adopted existing theory and accepted its constraints1.

Those constraints cannot be ignored. Although general relativity permits wormhole geometries mathematically, maintaining a macroscopic, traversable throat would require forms of negative energy density not known to exist in nature4. No empirical evidence for macroscopic negative energy densities has been observed as of yet. However, the film did not resolve these difficulties, instead described the wormhole as an artificial object placed near Saturn, implicitly acknowledging that its existence would require intervention beyond currently understood physics.

Interstellar was not only influenced by the physics, but it contributed to real research. In order to visualise curved spacetime accurately, the production relied on detailed relativistic ray-tracing calculations that later formed the basis of published research6. This required building computational tools that were capable of tracing the light paths through extreme spacetime curvature. The tools used were suitable for research-level work and even produced publishable scientific results! The relationship between physics and fiction was therefore not one-sided. While Interstellar didn't change underlying theory, it channelled funding and computational power into modelling techniques for gravitational lensing that bridged cinema and astrophysics. In doing so, it showed how science fiction can accelerate real scientific output.

Interstellar does not present a wormhole as effortless or inevitable. It presents it as something permitted by equations that have been tested, debated, and refined for over a century. In doing so, it illustrates that scientific development and science fiction are not opposing forces, but part of the same ongoing conversation, one in which fiction can clarify theory just as theory constrains fiction.
