Case Study 1: From the Earth to the Moon, and Verne's Columbiad

The Columbiad

Projectile motion – specifically, attaining a safe acceleration- is the foundational physical limiting factor for cannon-based manned space travel.

Before exploring Verne’s lunar cannon, we briefly examine the technical principles of firing a cannon [1] , which broadly apply from medieval smoothbore guns to the highly powered artillery of the American Civil War, the backdrop to From the Earth to the Moon.

1. Loading the cannon

The cannon is, in effect, a hollow metal cylinder. The projectile – a cannonball or artillery shell – is placed into the barrel. A propellant is loaded behind it – into the breech of the gun – to provide the energy and force for firing. Up until the 1880s, black powder (a mixture of potassium nitrate, charcoal and sulfur in the ratio 75:15:10) was commonly used. In Verne’s Columbiad, fulminating cotton serves as the fictional explosive.

2. Ignition

The powder is ignited via a fuse or friction primer. Rapid combustion produces expanded gases, generating high pressure behind the projectile. An electric spark is used to fire the Columbiad.

3. Projectile acceleration

The expanding gases push the projectile through the barrel. It’s acceleration is variable along the barrels length, governed by a variety of factors including the barrel’s dimensions, the pressure of the gases, and, in turn, the propellant used.

4. Ejection

Upon leaving the barrel, the projectile is no longer acted upon by the pressure of the propellant’s gases. It enters freefall, subject solely to gravity and air resistance.

5. Recoil

Newton’s third law dictates that as the projectile gains forward momentum, the cannon itself gains backward momentum. Historically, this was managed by the cannon carriage and crew – since the cannon has a very large mass, the velocity it acquires is much smaller than that of the cannonball. In the case of Verne’s Columbiad, which is installed directly into the Earth, it triggers a dramatic Earthquake.

The discharge of the Columbiad was accompanied by a perfect earthquake. Florida was shaken to its very depths. The gases of the powder, expanded by heat, forced back the atmospheric strata with tremendous violence, and this artificial hurricane rushed like a water-spout through the air.

Not a single spectator remained on his feet! Men, women children, all lay prostrate like ears of corn under a tempest. There ensued a terrible tumult; a large number of persons were seriously injured. J. T. Maston, who, despite all dictates of prudence, had kept in advance of the mass, was pitched back 120 feet, shooting like a projectile over the heads of his fellow-citizens. Three hundred thousand persons remained deaf for a time, and as though struck stupefied.

Verne’s cannon is a smoothbore – it lacks rifling. Rifling involves machining helical grooves inside the barrel, which impart spin to the projectile and help maintain its stability in flight. Thus, Verne’s cannon is very straightforward in construction indeed. It is a hollow cylinder, made of caste iron embedded deep into the ground.

Given that the projectile experiences variable acceleration both inside the canon and in free flight (due in part to air resistance), constructing an exact mathematical model is extremely challenging [2]

Danger of high G

Extensive research has been conducted into the physiological effects of extreme accelerations on humans.

Stoll's G-time curve demonstrates the dangers posed by sustained acceleration above approximately 3G. Humans can tolerate high G-forces, but only for very short durations.

This raises the central question:

What acceleration would a cannon impose?

Given that high G-forces are tolerable only briefly, it is reasonable to consider the maximum acceleration experienced by the projectile when assessing the feasibility of manned cannon-based space travel.

To estimate this, we consider a highly idealised cannon. In this model, the projectile undergoes uniform acceleration from rest to Earth's escape velocity.

Reaching escape velocity is essential: only at this velocity does the projectile possess sufficient kinetic energy to overcome the Earth's gravitational pull.

This is not a physically realistic cannon, per se. Gas expansion does not produce uniform acceleration for a given barrel length. However , this idealised case provides a lower bound on peak acceleration for a given barrel length. Any real cannon would subject the projectile to even greater maximum accelerations.

It also neglects air resistance which would be a major mitigating factor. In the real world, where there is air resistance, a velocity of 11.2km/s is not sufficient to escape as some energy will be lost working against that resistance.

Using the SUVAT equation:

\[ v^2 = u^2 + 2as \]

Where:

  • \( v \) = escape velocity (11.2km/s)
  • \( u \) = initial velocity (taken to be zero)
  • \( a \) = acceleration inside the barrel
  • \( s \) = barrel length

Rearranging:

\[ a = \frac{v^2}{2s} \]

Thus, the acceleration experienced in this idealised limiting case is proportional to the square of the escape velocity and inversely proportional to the cannon length.

If we want low Gs, we need a long cannon... But just how long?

The graph below visualises just that. Above 10G has been taken to be "Probably fatal", 3G-10G has been taken to be "Highly dangerous" and 1G-3G is "Likely safe". Survivability is dependent on a wide array of factors including individual physical health. Thus, this is not definitive, but the examination of the G-time graph shows that it is reasonable.

Some stats for reference:

Maximum space shuttle G-force during launch and re-entry: 3G

Apollo 16 G-force on re-entry: 7.19G

Fighter pilot G-force on seat ejection: 15G-25G

To achieve a G-force of 3G, a safe-ish G-force, the cannon must be over 2000km long. So, for a space launcher to be anywhere near human grade, it must be at least of the order of the 1000s of kilometres long. The Earth has an average radius of 6371km. These are astronomically large cannons we're talking about.

Does Verne address the issue of dangerous acceleration?

Yes, Verne does attempt to address this issue. He attempts to cushion the launch by implementing a water-based raft system into the projectile [4]:

The projectile had now to be filled to the depth of three feet with a bed of water, intended to support a water-tight wooden disc, which worked easily within the walls of the projectile. It was upon this kind of raft that the travelers were to take their place. This body of water was divided by horizontal partitions, which the shock of the departure would have to break in succession. Then each sheet of the water, from the lowest to the highest, running off into escape tubes toward the top of the projectile, constituted a kind of spring; and the wooden disc, supplied with extremely powerful plugs, could not strike the lowest plate except after breaking successively the different partitions. Undoubtedly the travelers would still have to encounter a violent recoil after the complete escapement of the water; but the first shock would be almost entirely destroyed by this powerful spring. The upper parts of the walls were lined with a thick padding of leather, fastened upon springs of the best steel, behind which the escape tubes were completely concealed; thus all imaginable precautions had been taken for averting the first shock; and if they did get crushed, they must, as Michel Ardan said, be made of very bad materials.

Despite this attempt at sci-fi realism, for Verne is correct and wise to address this fatal issue, Verne largely fails. It still stands that the distance over which the astronauts are accelerated is small. And thus, the acceleration will be astronomically large. In order for the cushioning to have any meaningful effect with regards to survival, it must take place over 100s of kilometres - the capsule would necessarily be much longer than the cannon itself.

Columbiad: The Verdict

In accordance with the idealised mathematics, Verne's passengers would have experienced a minimum of about 50,000G - this would be fatal.

Physically, his work is impressive as he does outline what it would take to launch a large invincible mass to the moon. He is, however, limited by human physiology and frailty.
