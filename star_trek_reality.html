The Alcubierre Equation

Alcubierre attempted in his 1994 publication to find a mathematical way to express the warp drive6. Theoretically, this could show what physical properties a warp drive would have, pointing the way towards developing a working model (in reality, there are a few problems with this, see the next page, 'Fact or Fiction'). The details behind his full derivation use elements of General Relativity, but it is possible to explore the ideas Alcubierre introduced by looking at the following, much simpler function:

$f(r_s) = \frac{tanh(\sigma (r_s + R)) - tanh(\sigma (r_s - R))}{2 \cdot tanh(\sigma R)}$

This can be thought of as describing the shape of the warp bubble, where the flat exterior and interior represent normal spacetime (without and within the bubble respectively), and the curve between represents the curved spacetime of the bubble walls. Alcubierre developed a series of metrics – equations defining how to calculate distances between objects – which describe the properties of the warp drive, such as the spatial geometry and velocity components. This function $f(r_s)$ is used in the metric that describes 'the velocity profile of the warp bubble along the x-axis' – that is, how spacetime moves relative to an outside observer. So if we were to look at a warp bubble, this function describes the geometry of what we would see.

The top hat shape function can be observed by plotting this on a graph. The function outputs a value between 0 and 1. Physically, when it is 0, it means that there is no change to space time – i.e., outside the bubble – and at 1, which occurs inside the bubble, the full effect of the warp is experienced. The values inbetween represent the bubble wall, where the transition from normal to shifted spacetime occurs. The \sigma parameter controls the magnitude of spacetime curvature as it defines how gradually the shift from 0 to 1 occurs, which has a profound effect on the energy required for the bubble (see 'Fact or Fiction'). It is important to note that this is more 'descriptive mathematics', rather than derived equations: the functions were selected to produce the desired shape, rather than coming out of earlier theory. The variables $\sigma$ and $r_s$ can also be thought of as arbitrary, used to change the shape of the function. Alcubierre's motivation behind developing this shape function was that it is the simplest way to define two distinct uncurved regions graphically, but other more complex shapes have been proposed as being more feasible in terms of energy requirements.11
