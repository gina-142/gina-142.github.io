Physical Primers

So - you want to escape Earth and fly into space.

Before we can venture into the deep black, we need a little physics. What follows are three conceptual primers underpinning both real-world rocketry and its depiction in science fiction:

- Escape velocity

- The rocket equation

- Time dilation

The first two govern real spaceflight. The latter applies only to speculative fiction - it is relevant only when velocities approach the speed of light.

In all cases, we consider highly idealised systems. We are interested in physical plausibility, not technical feasibility. The latter is what makes spaceflight so difficult; science fiction is often content to bypass this.

Gravity imposes an energy cost on spaceflight. Momentum imposes exponential scaling and a technical faff. Physica and the fabric of spacetime resist narrative desires.

Escape velocity: Escaping the gravitational well

To venture forth into the final frontier, a spacecraft must reach what is known as an escape velocity – the minimum speed required to avoid falling back down to Earth under gravity.

In principle, this is fairly straightforward – simple Newtonian physics, basic algebraic manipulation and easy to intuit. In practice, it is one of the greatest engineering challenges humanity has ever faced – and conquered.

The fact that it took until 1957 until the first man-made objects to achieve escape velocity – two small pellet sized probes – is testament to the tight grip of these technical constraints. Orbiting is difficult – escaping is much harder still.

Energy: The cosmic accounting system

We will need to consider the two forms of energy that determine whether we break free from the terrestrial chains: Kinetic energy (K.E) and gravitational potential energy (G.P.E).

It does not matter how you conceptualise these two quantities – the numbers and the maths are what count here!

Kinetic energy (K.E.)

An object in motion possesses kinetic energy:

\[ K.E. = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \]

Where:

  • \( m \) = object's mass
  • \( v \) = object velocity

In our case, \( v \) is the upward speed of the spacecraft.

Gravitational Potential Energy (G.P.E.).

An object possesses gravitational potential energy by virtue of its position in a gravitational field, produced by the Earth. It is equivalent to the energy you would need to expend to separate the object and Earth to infinity. It is given by:

\[ G.P.E. = - \frac{GMm}{r} \]

Where:

  • \( M \) = Earth's mass
  • \( m \) = object mass
  • \( r \) = distance of the object from the Earth's (or applicable planet's) centre
  • \( G \) = the gravitational constant – a universal value that governs the strength of gravity across the cosmos

The negative sign matters here. We define gravitational potential energy to be zero at infinite distance. At the Earth’s surface, it is negative – meaning energy must be supplied to escape.

Conservation of energy

Abstracting further, energy is simply a number that remains constant. For our spacecraft, its total energy is simply the sum of its gravitational potential energy and its kinetic energy:

\[ Total Energy = K.E. + G.P.E. = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 - \frac{GMm}{r} \]

As the spacecraft rises:

  • Its gravitational potential energy increases – thus becoming less negative.
  • Its kinetic energy decreases to balance this.

You may think transactionally: Kinetic energy is “spent” climbing out of Earth’s gravitational well and converted to potential energy.

For escape, the spacecraft must have a non-negative total energy:

\[ 0 \le \frac{1}{2} m v_{escape}^2 - \frac{GMm}{r} \]

Algebraic manipulation yields:

\[ v_{escape} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}} \]

This is the escape velocity. Notice how it is independent of the spacecraft’s mass. A feather and a Saturn V rocket require the same escape speed (assuming an idealised scenario with no air resistance). Any velocity higher than this may escape.

Physics is democratic. Engineering is not.

For the Earth

\[ v_{escape} = 11.2 \text{km/s} \]

That’s very fast. And, indeed, this is a vast underestimate, for to actually escape the Earth’s gravitational vice, one must overcome air resistance and other non-gravitational factors.

Where science fiction cheats:

In classic space opera, ships effortlessly drift from planet to planet with limited acceleration phase. In reality, enormous energy expenditure is required to just leave the ground.

Harder science fiction may depict this.

Escape velocity is thus the first tyranny of space.
