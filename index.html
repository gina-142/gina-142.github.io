Physics and the Final Frontier: A historical survey of spaceflight in science fiction

MISSION STATEMENT:

Greetings, traveller.

Spaceflight is a modern expression of the long tradition of human boundary pushing. From Christopher Columbus to Roald Amundsen, to Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin, the restless human spirit has continuously driven us towards unknown frontiers. Ocean and ice have been substituted for vacuum and stardust.

Science fiction continues in this vein. In some instances, it fantastically pushes the boundaries of the imagination, but in others it seriously attempts extrapolation from the scientific status quo. With various degrees of rigour, sci-fi offers us valuable insight into what we could be. Some consider spaceflight to be a cool, peripheral thing, but others frame it – as in 2001: A Space Odyssey, or those who subscribe to a radical form of Turner's Frontier Thesis – as a natural and necessary component of human evolution.

Historical analysis itself is neither neutral nor objective. It is a human process of heuristic and narrative construction. As explored by former Chief Historian at NASA Dr. Roger D. Launius, with whom we join for an extended conversation, our conception of historical “turning points” are often misguided. Those moments which we take to be foundational, are very often not.

This project constructs a consciously subjective narrative examining how spaceflight, as depicted within science fiction, has evolved alongside the real thing. We focus on three key case studies:

  1. Early scientific realism and the work of Jules Verne.
  2. The mid-20th Century space western, Star Trek.
  3. Contemporary speculative but grounded realism of Interstellar.

Within each case study, we examine the physics underlying methods of space travel. We explore both the mathematical foundations and the physical plausibility of such modes of exploration. From the liquifying acceleration of Verne's Columbiad – examined through the lens of Newtonian physics – to Kip Thorne's theoretical work underpinning Interstellar's wormhole, we are asking three key questions:

  1. How does the fictitious method of spaceflight work?
  2. Is it physically realistic?
  3. What is the historical and cultural context of this method, and what were the creators aims?

We hope you will join us on this journey!

Space: the final frontier. These are the voyages of the starship Enterprise. Its five-year mission: to explore strange new worlds; to seek out new life and new civilizations; to boldly go where no man has gone before!
